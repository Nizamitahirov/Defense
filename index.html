Section I · The Problem 04 / 25

The core HRM problem

Three failures of a sales-only PMS

Each failure produces a different kind of damage — to fairness, to motivation, and to strategy.

No. 01

Contextual injustice

Managers in high-traffic, large-format locations look like superstars because of where they stand, not how they lead. Equally talented managers in smaller formats are penalised for context they did not choose.

"Sales reward the store, not the manager."

No. 02

The comfort zone

With no behavioural differentiation in evaluation, the marginal incentive to excel beyond minimum effort collapses. Managers converge on the same uniform pattern — and the system has no language to tell them apart.

"If everyone is rated the same, no one has reason to lead differently."

No. 03

Strategic misalignment

Waste control, OPEX discipline and staff turnover — the levers most tied to long-term financial health — are not in the evaluation. Manager actions are decoupled from the outcomes Bravo most needs to protect.

"What is measured is not what matters."

Section II · Literature 06 / 25

Theoretical foundations

Three lenses for looking beyond the
sales figure

Financial Myopia

Madhani 2021 · Vuong & Nguyen 2022

Short-term sales are a lagging indicator. Managing only to that figure conceals the leading indicators — waste, retention, customer service — that drive future performance, and trains managers to optimise for a past, not a future, business.

Store vs. Manager Effect

Metcalfe, Sollaci & Syverson 2023

Managers account for an estimated 25–35% of productivity variance across stores. Swapping a weak manager for a strong one can lift store productivity by up to 50% — without changing infrastructure. The system has to separate skill from site.

Balanced Scorecard

Kaplan & Norton 1996

Performance is best read through four perspectives: financial, customer, internal process, learning & growth. Financial measures alone are insufficient; non-financial KPIs are the drivers of the financial KPIs that follow.

Section II · Literature 07 / 25

Operational metrics retail science recognises

The "invisible" indicators of
managerial quality

OPEX management

Stoyanov 2021

Operating Expense Ratio is the cleanest quantitative read on a manager's internal discipline — the ability to deliver the same output with less input, without sacrificing service or staff.

Waste & stock loss

Riesenegger et al. 2023

Expiry rates and shrink trace directly to replenishment policy and shelf planning. These are the invisible leaks the P&L hides — and the leaks closest to a manager's direct control.

Staff turnover

Carr 2025 · DeSilva 2021

Retention is leadership made visible. High-performing managers hold teams together, preserve store-floor knowledge, and avoid the hidden hiring cost that quietly erodes the operating margin.

"Moving to a multifactorial framework is not a technical tweak. It is a strategic shift toward long-term organisational resilience." — Carr (2025)

Section IV · Findings 17 / 25

S9 + S10 · thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke)

What managers say about the barriers

THEME T1 · DATA INACCESSIBILITY

"We cannot influence what we cannot see."

"Neticelerden xeberimiz olmur. Real neticeyi deyiq bilmirem."

Managers don't have a live view of their own operational performance. The MPI must come with a dashboard, or it cannot ask managers to manage to it.

THEME T2 · UNCLEAR PRIORITIES

"Prioritetlerin belli olmamasi"

"KPI-larin aydin izahi" — clearer explanation of KPIs, with explicit weights.

Without published weights and explicit definitions, managers are asked to optimise something they cannot reason about. The MPI fixes this by publishing the weights up front.

THEME T3 · EXCLUSION FROM DECISIONS

"Store managers are not included in decisions."

"Qerarlarin verilmesinde magaza mudirinin istirak etmemesi."

A participatory design — where managers' S8 rankings shape the MPI weights — converts a top-down system into one that has been authored, in part, by the people it evaluates.

THEME T4 · STAFFING INSTABILITY

"Frequent staff changes prevent stable execution."

"Kadr catishmazligi ve ya tez-tez kadr deyisikliyi…"

Turnover is both a cause of operational shortfall and a metric — which is why it sits explicitly inside the MPI's operational group.

Beyond Sales · Defense Deck 19 — MPI Framework
Section V · The Solution 20 / 25

Scoring logic & normalisation

Fair to the manager, transparent
to the business

I.

A 20% tolerance band

Every ratio KPI gets a buffer before any penalty applies. Small deviations from target are not punished — managers are protected from external noise they cannot control.

if result ≤ target → score = 1.0
II.

Linear deduction

Beyond the tolerance, the score drops linearly to zero at 120% of target. No cliff-edges, no perverse incentives — the system rewards proximity to target rather than crossing a single threshold.

if result ≥ target × 1.2 → score = 0
III.

Unified 0 – 100 scale

All seven KPIs — whether expressed as ratios, percentages or a 1-to-5 rating — are normalised to a single 0–100 score before weighting. Group 1 and Group 2 are then combined into the final MPI.

MPI = G1 × 0.50 + G2 × 0.50

Section VI · Conclusion 25 / 25

Key strategic takeaways

From a sales culture to a culture
of operational excellence

01
Sales is a poor proxy
Sales performance has no significant correlation with the operational metrics most under a manager's direct control. Sales reward the store, not the manager.
02
Invisible leaks become visible
Waste, OPEX, turnover and customer experience cluster into a single operational discipline factor — and the MPI is the first place at Bravo where that factor is priced.
03
Structural bias removed
Within-format ranking ends the contextual injustice of a single network-wide leaderboard. Managers compete with peers, not with their site.
04
A long-term strategic shift
More than a metric change — a move toward a more sustainable, customer-centric retail operation, with manager incentives aligned to Bravo's long-term financial health.
